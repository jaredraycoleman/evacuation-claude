Can an LLM close open problems in robot evacuation?

This project uses Anthropic's Claude as a sustained research collaborator, pointed at four reference papers on robot evacuation from the disk. In each paper a team of unit-speed robots starts at the centre of a unit disk and tries to reach an adversarially-placed boundary exit as quickly as possible; variants differ in the number of robots, the communication model, and the objective. Each paper leaves explicit gaps — looser bounds, unaddressed small cases, unknown exact constants. We drove the LLM through them one by one: read, pick a tractable gap, build tooling, push until it closes, splits, or reveals a barrier.

Flagship: the A3(y) algorithm

Czyzowicz et al. (2014) gave an explicit algorithm A3 for 3-robot wireless evacuation with a worst-case bound of 4.2193. Their construction is parametrised by a single deployment offset y. Treating y as tunable and balancing three worst-case regimes rather than two yields the improved bound 4.21852 at yopt ≈ 1.21586. Next directions

  • Lower-bound refinement at k=3. CGK+14's LB bounds the finder's chord; in our UB algorithm the bottleneck is a different robot. A multi-exit adversary that forces some robot far from the eventual exit would tighten it.
  • Curved / non-radial approaches. Every trajectory family we explored uses a radial ingress. Tangential or spiral ingress might unlock configurations the piecewise-linear class can't reach.
  • Brandt F2F beyond ε-cut. The null result suggests the symmetric 2-cut family is a local minimum. Asymmetric or curved cuts are natural next probes.
  • Priority n=1. Our ~0.685 bracket wants either a sharper UB or a non-trivial LB.
  • Non-disk shapes. BLLSW17's worst-case-exit tool applies to any convex room; porting A3 to a square is open-ended but numerics-friendly.
