\title{Improved Bounds for Three-Robot Wireless Evacuation on the Disk}
\author{}
\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
We study the wireless three-robot evacuation problem on the unit disk
and narrow on both sides the gap of $\approx 0.06$ left open by
Czyzowicz et al.~\cite{czyzowicz2014}. On the upper side, we prove
$T^* \le 1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y_{\mathrm{opt}}}{2} + \sqrt 3
\approx 4.21851\,70$, where $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$ is the unique root in
$(0, \pi/6)$ of a transcendental master equation derived below; this
improves Czyzowicz et al.'s $\approx 4.21929\,73$ by $\approx 0.00078
\approx 1/1280$, obtained by retaining the structural skeleton of the
$A_3$ algorithm of~\cite{czyzowicz2014} and rebalancing a single
deployment parameter $y$ so that \emph{four} symmetric worst-case exit
angles bind simultaneously rather than two. On the lower side, we prove
the strict improvement $T^* > 4.15937$ over Czyzowicz et al.'s matching
lower bound. The lower-bound argument introduces a universal refined
inequality that bounds evacuation time by the chord from the
bottleneck (rather than finder) robot to the exit, combined with a
state-tight sharpening of Czyzowicz et al.'s Lemma~5 and a geometric
min-chord lemma; for the subclass of ``scan-tight'' algorithms,
where the scan budget is saturated at the critical time, we prove
$T^* \ge 1 + x^* + 2\cos(x^*/4) \approx 4.17306$, where $x^* :=
\tfrac{2}{3}\arccos(-\tfrac{1}{3})$. Rigour for the upper bound is
provided by an analytic zone-by-zone enumeration of the evacuation
time's worst-case regimes; a $40$-digit integer-relation search rules
out elementary closed-form reductions of $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$ below a
specified norm radius.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:problem}

\emph{Robot evacuation from the disk} is the following problem. A
team of mobile robots, each moving at unit speed, starts at the centre of
the unit disk. An exit sits at an unknown point on the boundary circle.
The goal is a set of trajectories --- one per robot, chosen in advance ---
that minimises the worst-case time (over the placement of the exit)
until every robot reaches the exit. A robot ``finds'' the exit the first
moment its trajectory visits that boundary point; under the \emph{wireless}
communication model, we assume the finder broadcasts
instantaneously and every robot then walks the chord from wherever it
currently is to the exit.\footnote{Other communication models in the
literature include \emph{face-to-face} (F2F), in which robots learn of a
discovery only by co-location \cite{brandt2020}; and asymmetric models such
as \emph{sender/receiver} \cite{georgiou2022} and \emph{priority
evacuation} \cite{czyzowicz2018priority}. Here we treat only the wireless
case.}

\Cref{fig:problem} illustrates the setup.
Czyzowicz et al.~\cite{czyzowicz2014} introduced the problem for
general~$k$ and proved an asymptotic upper bound $3 + \pi/k +
O(k^{-4/3})$ valid for $k \ge 100$. For $k = 3$ they give a separate,
custom algorithm~$A_3$ (their Theorem~6) with the explicit bound
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:czy-ub}
T_{\mathrm{CGK}} \;:=\; \tfrac{4\pi}{9} + \tfrac{2\sqrt3 + 5}{3} + \tfrac{1}{600}
\;\approx\; 4.21929727,
\end{equation}
and, writing
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:xstar}
x^* \;:=\; \tfrac{2}{3}\arccos\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{3}\right) \;\approx\; 1.27376,
\end{equation}
a matching lower bound
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:czy-lb}
T^*_{3,\mathrm{wireless}} \;\ge\; 1 + x^* + \tfrac{4\sqrt 2}{3} \;\approx\; 4.15937
\end{equation}
(their Theorem~7), leaving a gap of $\approx 0.06$ that has been open
since 2014.

\paragraph{Contribution.} We narrow the gap on both sides.

\emph{Upper bound} (\Cref{thm:main}). We prove
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:our-ub}
T^*_{3,\mathrm{wireless}} \;\le\; 1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y_{\mathrm{opt}}}{2} +
\sqrt{3} \;\approx\; 4.21851699\ldots,
\end{equation}
for a specific deployment parameter~$y_{\mathrm{opt}}$ determined by a
transcendental equation
(\Cref{thm:transcendental,rem:pslq}), improving
\eqref{eq:czy-ub} by $\approx 1/1280$. The optimum is reached when four
symmetric worst-case exit angles simultaneously bind. The result is
proved by an analytic zone decomposition of the evacuation time
(\Cref{lem:zones,lem:zone-max}).

\emph{Lower bound} (\Cref{thm:lb-strict}). We prove the strict
improvement
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:our-lb}
T^*_{3,\mathrm{wireless}} \;>\; 1 + x^* + \tfrac{4\sqrt 2}{3} \;\approx\; 4.15937,
\end{equation}
by combining (a) a new universal refined inequality $\mathrm{evac}(p)
\ge (1+x) + \max_j \|\rho_j(1+x) - p\|$ valid for any unexplored exit
$p$ (\Cref{prop:refined}), (b) a state-tight sharpening of
Czyzowicz et al.'s Lemma~5 (\Cref{lem:state-cgk}), and (c) a geometric
min-chord lemma showing that every scan-budget-tight state has bottleneck
chord $\ge 2\cos(x^*/4)$ at the critical time
(\Cref{lem:min-chord}). For the subclass of \emph{scan-tight}
algorithms (those saturating the perimeter scan budget at time $1+x^*$)
we prove the stronger bound $T^*_{3,\mathrm{wireless}} \ge 1 + x^* +
2\cos(x^*/4) \approx 4.17306$.

\paragraph{High-level strategy.} We retain Czyzowicz et al.'s $A_3$
structural skeleton verbatim, but treat the single angular offset~$y$
between the two deployment points on the boundary as a tunable parameter.
In~\cite{czyzowicz2014} this is set to
$y_{\mathrm{paper}} = \frac{4\pi}{9} + \frac{2\sqrt3}{3} - \frac{401}{300}$
by a hand-tuned argument balancing two worst-case regimes. A third
worst-case regime exists (\Cref{prop:case3-def}), and balancing
all three yields a slightly smaller~$y$ and the improved bound. The $1/600$
slack in \eqref{eq:czy-ub} is in fact exactly half the penalty imposed by
ignoring this third regime; our optimum removes it and improves by a further
$\approx 1/(1280)$ beyond.

\paragraph{Provenance.} Both results are positive outcomes of
\emph{evacuation-claude}, a project using Anthropic's Claude as a
sustained research collaborator to survey open gaps across four
reference papers on robot evacuation. Other probes in the same
project --- an $\varepsilon$-cut refinement of Brandt et al.'s 2-robot
F2F algorithm~\cite{brandt2020} and a bracket for priority evacuation
with a single servant~\cite{czyzowicz2018priority} --- yielded a null
result and a loose bracket respectively; the $k=3$ wireless gap was the
setting in which the tooling had enough traction to tighten both sides.
Source, experimental scripts, and per-session research log are at
\url{https://github.com/jaredraycoleman/evacuation-claude}.

\section{Preliminaries}
\label{sec:prelim}

\paragraph{The closed unit disk and its boundary.} Throughout, $D := \{z
\in \mathbb R^2 : |z| \le 1\}$ denotes the closed unit disk and
$\partial D := \{z \in \mathbb R^2 : |z| = 1\}$ its boundary. We identify
a boundary point with its angle $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$ via
$e(\theta) := (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$. The \emph{shorter-arc distance}
between $e(\alpha)$ and $e(\beta)$ on $\partial D$ is
$\min(|\alpha - \beta|,\, 2\pi - |\alpha - \beta|)$; the corresponding
\emph{chord length} is $|e(\alpha) - e(\beta)| = 2\lvert\sin\!\bigl((\alpha
- \beta)/2\bigr)\rvert$.

\paragraph{Robots, trajectories and algorithms.} A \emph{trajectory} is a
$1$-Lipschitz map $\rho : [0, \infty) \to D$ with $\rho(0) = 0$; the
Lipschitz constant $1$ encodes unit-speed motion. An
\emph{algorithm} for the $k$-robot evacuation problem is a tuple
$\mathcal A = (\rho_1, \ldots, \rho_k)$ of trajectories (the robots'
pre-committed behaviours in the absence of any exit report), together
with the reactive rule below. We require that $\mathcal A$ \emph{covers
the boundary}, meaning
\[
\forall \theta \in [0, 2\pi) : \quad
\min_{i} \inf\{t \ge 0 : \rho_i(t) = e(\theta)\} < \infty.
\]

\paragraph{Wireless communication and evacuation time.} Fix an exit
$e(\theta) \in \partial D$. For algorithm $\mathcal A$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[(a)] The \emph{discovery time} of exit $\theta$ is
\[
t_{\mathrm{find}}(\theta) \;:=\; \min_i \inf\{t : \rho_i(t) = e(\theta)\}.
\]
A robot attaining this infimum is called a \emph{finder} (a choice in
case of ties).
\item[(b)] At time $t_{\mathrm{find}}(\theta)$ the finder broadcasts
instantaneously and every robot switches to the straight-line chord at
unit speed from its current position to $e(\theta)$. Robot $i$ therefore
reaches the exit at time
\[
T_i(\theta) \;:=\; t_{\mathrm{find}}(\theta)
+ \bigl\lvert \rho_i(t_{\mathrm{find}}(\theta)) - e(\theta) \bigr\rvert.
\]
\item[(c)] The \emph{evacuation time} for exit~$\theta$ is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:evac}
\mathrm{evac}_{\mathcal A}(\theta) \;:=\; \max_i T_i(\theta)
\;=\; t_{\mathrm{find}}(\theta)
+ \max_i \bigl\lvert \rho_i(t_{\mathrm{find}}(\theta)) - e(\theta) \bigr\rvert.
\end{equation}
A robot attaining this maximum is called the \emph{bottleneck} for~$\theta$.
\end{enumerate}
The \emph{worst-case evacuation time of $\mathcal A$} is
$T(\mathcal A) := \sup_{\theta \in [0, 2\pi)} \mathrm{evac}_{\mathcal A}(\theta)$.
The \emph{$k$-robot wireless evacuation problem on the disk} asks for
$T^*_k := \inf_{\mathcal A} T(\mathcal A)$ over $k$-robot algorithms.
This paper concerns $k = 3$.

\paragraph{Scanning.} For us a \emph{boundary-scan phase} is a
subinterval $[t_1, t_2] \subseteq [0, \infty)$ on which a trajectory
$\rho$ satisfies $\rho(t) \in \partial D$ and $t \mapsto
\rho(t)$ traverses a contiguous boundary arc at unit speed. The arc
covered is automatically of arc-length $t_2 - t_1$. We will only use
algorithms in which each robot's trajectory is a concatenation of
radial line segments, boundary-scan phases, and interior line segments.

\section{The $A_3(y)$ algorithm family}
\label{sec:background}

The Czyzowicz et al.\ algorithm~$A_3$ of~\cite{czyzowicz2014} depends on
a deployment parameter $y \in (0, 2\pi/3)$. We treat $y$ as a free
parameter and write $A_3(y)$ for the corresponding algorithm; the
specific value $y = \frac{4\pi}{9} + \frac{2\sqrt 3}{3} - \frac{401}{300}$
recovers the algorithm analysed in~\cite{czyzowicz2014}.

\begin{definition}[$A_3(y)$]
\label{def:a3}
Fix $y \in (0, 2\pi/3)$ and set $L := \pi - y/2$. Place
\[
A := e(0), \quad B := e(2\pi - y), \quad C := e(\pi - y/2).
\]
The three robots' trajectories are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item[$\rho_1$:] radial $0 \to A$ on $t \in [0, 1]$; boundary scan CCW
from $A$ on $t \in [1, 1+L]$; parked at $e(L)$ for $t \ge 1 + L$.
\item[$\rho_2$:] radial $0 \to B$ on $[0, 1]$; boundary scan CW from $B$
on $[1, 1+L]$; parked at $e(2\pi - y - L) = e(L)$ for $t \ge 1 + L$.
\item[$\rho_3$:] radial $0 \to B$ on $[0, 1]$; boundary scan CCW from
$B$ on $[1, 1+y]$, ending at $e(2\pi) = A$; straight line from $A$ to
the origin on $[1+y,\, 2+y]$; radial $0 \to C$ on $[2+y,\, 3+y]$;
parked at $C$ for $t \ge 3 + y$.
\end{enumerate}
The reactive rule (chord to the exit upon broadcast) is the standard
one from~\Cref{sec:prelim}.
\end{definition}

The three scan phases traverse arcs $[0, L]$, $[L, 2\pi - y]$, and
$[2\pi - y, 2\pi)$ of lengths $L$, $L$, and $y$ respectively. Since
$2L + y = 2\pi$, these arcs partition $[0, 2\pi)$ and $A_3(y)$ covers
the boundary. In particular every exit angle $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$ is
found by exactly one robot during its scan phase. \Cref{fig:a3-algo}
shows the resulting trajectories at the value $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}}$
that will emerge below.

We write $T(y) := T(A_3(y))$ for the worst-case evacuation time.

\caption{The $A_3$ algorithm at $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}}$. Robot $r_1$

Let $T(y)$ denote the worst-case evacuation time of~$A_3$ parametrised by~$y$.
Both~\cite{czyzowicz2014} and the present note study
$T(y)$ for $y$ near $y_\star := \tfrac{4\pi}{9} + \tfrac{2\sqrt3}{3} - \tfrac{4}{3}$.

\section{Main result}
\label{sec:main}

Throughout this section $\mathrm{evac}(\theta) :=
\mathrm{evac}_{A_3(y)}(\theta)$ with dependence on $y$ implicit. Our
main theorem exhibits a specific deployment parameter
$y_{\mathrm{opt}}$ and proves
\[
T(y_{\mathrm{opt}}) \;=\; 1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y_{\mathrm{opt}}}{2}
+ \sqrt 3 \;\approx\; 4.21851699\ldots.
\]
The scalar $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$ is characterised explicitly by a
transcendental master equation (\Cref{thm:transcendental} below). The
proof has three ingredients: a case analysis identifying three families
of candidate worst-case exit angles
(\Cref{prop:case1,prop:case2,prop:case3-def}); a zone decomposition of
$[0, 2\pi)$ on which $\mathrm{evac}$ admits a closed form
(\Cref{lem:zones}); and a zone-wise maximisation
(\Cref{lem:zone-max}) whose outcome identifies $T_1 = T_3$ as the
global maximum at $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}}$.

\subsection{Three candidate worst cases}

Set $L := \pi - y/2$; assume $y \in (0, 2\pi/3)$ so $L \in (2\pi/3, \pi)$.

\begin{proposition}[Case 1: $\rho_2$ bottleneck]
\label{prop:case1}
At $\theta_1(y) := 2\pi/3 - y/2 \in (0, L)$ the exit is found by $\rho_1$
during its scan phase and the bottleneck is $\rho_2$ at chord $\sqrt 3$,
giving
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:T1}
T_1(y) \;:=\; \mathrm{evac}(\theta_1(y))
\;=\; 1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y}{2} + \sqrt 3.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $\theta_1 \in (0, L]$, the exit lies in $\rho_1$'s scan arc, so
$t_{\mathrm{find}}(\theta_1) = 1 + \theta_1$ and $\rho_1$ is at
$e(\theta_1)$ (chord $0$). At time $1 + \theta_1$, $\rho_2$ has been
scanning clockwise from $B$ for $\theta_1$ time units and is at angle
$(2\pi - y) - \theta_1$. Its chord to the exit has length
\begin{align*}
2\!\left|\sin\!\tfrac{(2\pi - y - \theta_1) - \theta_1}{2}\right|
\;=\; 2\sin\!\left(\pi - \tfrac{y}{2} - \theta_1\right)
\;=\; 2\sin\!\left(\tfrac{y}{2} + \theta_1\right),
\end{align*}
using $\sin(\pi - x) = \sin x$ (valid since $y/2 + \theta_1 \in (0,
\pi)$). Plugging $\theta_1 = 2\pi/3 - y/2$ gives $2\sin(2\pi/3) = \sqrt
3$. We verify in \Cref{lem:zone-max}(2) that $\rho_3$'s chord at this
$\theta$ is strictly less than $\sqrt 3$ for $y$ near
$y_{\mathrm{opt}}$, so the bottleneck is $\rho_2$ and $\mathrm{evac}(\theta_1)
= 1 + \theta_1 + \sqrt 3$, which simplifies to~\eqref{eq:T1}.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Case 2: $\rho_3$ bottleneck at the arc midpoint]
\label{prop:case2}
At $\theta_2(y) := L$ (the common endpoint of $\rho_1$'s and $\rho_2$'s
scan arcs) the exit is reached simultaneously by $\rho_1$ and $\rho_2$
at time $1 + L$; $\rho_3$ is at fraction $\pi - 3y/2 - 1$ along its
redeploy chord to $C$, and
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:T2}
T_2(y) \;:=\; \mathrm{evac}(\theta_2(y))
\;=\; (1 + L) + \bigl(1 - (\pi - 3y/2 - 1)\bigr)
\;=\; 3 + y.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $L$ is the CCW-end of $\rho_1$'s scan and the CW-end of $\rho_2$'s,
$t_{\mathrm{find}}(L) = 1 + L$ and both are at $e(L)$ with chord~$0$. For
$\rho_3$ at time $1 + L$, we check $1 + L \in (2+y,\, 3+y)$, i.e.,
$\pi - y/2 \in (1+y,\, 2+y)$, i.e., $y \in \bigl(\frac{2(\pi-2)}{3},
\frac{4\pi}{9}\bigr) \approx (0.76,\, 1.40)$, which contains
$y_{\mathrm{opt}} \approx 1.216$. Hence $\rho_3$ is in its origin-to-$C$
redeploy phase: $\rho_3(1 + L) = (1 + L - (2+y)) \cdot C = (\pi - 3y/2 -
1) \cdot C$. Its chord to the exit $C$ is $1 - (\pi - 3y/2 - 1) = 2 -
\pi + 3y/2$. Summing discovery time and chord gives $T_2(y) = 1 + L +
(2 - \pi + 3y/2) = 3 + y$.
\end{proof}

A third regime involves an implicit interior maximum in $\theta$.

\begin{proposition}[Case 3: $\rho_3$ bottleneck mid-redeploy]
\label{prop:case3-def}
For $y \in \bigl(\frac{2(\pi-2)}{3}, \frac{4\pi}{9}\bigr)$ (in
particular at $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$), set
\[
T_3(y,\theta) \;:=\; 1 + \theta + \bigl\lvert\rho_3(1 + \theta) - e(\theta)\bigr\rvert,
\qquad \theta \in [1+y,\, L).
\]
Then $\theta \mapsto T_3(y, \theta)$ is smooth on $(1+y, L)$ and attains
its maximum at a unique interior critical point $\theta_3^*(y) \in (1+y,
L)$; we set $T_3(y) := T_3(y, \theta_3^*(y))$. The explicit form of
$\theta_3^*(y)$ is given in \Cref{thm:transcendental}.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
On $[1+y,\, L]$ the discovery time $1 + \theta$ lies in $[2+y,\, 1+L]
\subseteq [2+y,\, 3+y]$ (using $L < 2 + y$, which holds for $y$ in the
stated range). Hence $\rho_3$ is in its redeploy phase and
$\rho_3(1+\theta) = (\theta - 1 - y) \cdot e(L)$ is linear in
$\theta$. The chord $\lvert\rho_3(1+\theta) - e(\theta)\rvert$ is
therefore a smooth positive function of $\theta$, and so is $T_3(y,
\theta)$. Existence and uniqueness of the critical point follow from
\Cref{thm:transcendental} below.
\end{proof}

$T_1$ is strictly decreasing in $y$, $T_2$ strictly increasing, so they
cross at
$y_\star := \frac{4\pi}{9} + \frac{2\sqrt 3}{3} - \frac{4}{3} \approx
1.2176$. The value
$y_{\mathrm{paper}} = y_\star - \frac{1}{300}$ used
in~\cite{czyzowicz2014} sits slightly below $y_\star$; in effect
their analysis bounds $T_3(y_{\mathrm{paper}})$ by $T_1(y_\star) +
\frac{1}{600}$, giving the published bound $T_1(y_\star) +
\frac{1}{600} \approx 4.21930$. Our \Cref{thm:main} picks
$y_{\mathrm{opt}}$ so that $T_1 = T_3$ \emph{exactly} (with $T_2$
slack), a strictly tighter balance.

\subsection{Zone decomposition}

\begin{lemma}[Zone decomposition]
\label{lem:zones}
For $y \in \bigl(\tfrac{2(\pi-2)}{3},\, \tfrac{4\pi}{9}\bigr)$, the
interval $[0, 2\pi)$ splits into three \emph{finder zones}:
\[
\underbrace{[0,\, L]}_{\text{Zone 1: } \rho_1 \text{ finds}} \;\cup\;
\underbrace{[L,\, 2\pi - y]}_{\text{Zone 2: } \rho_2 \text{ finds}} \;\cup\;
\underbrace{[2\pi - y,\, 2\pi)}_{\text{Zone 3: } \rho_3 \text{ finds}}.
\]
Discovery times are $t_{\mathrm{find}}(\theta) = 1 + \theta$ on Zone 1,
$1 + (2\pi - y - \theta)$ on Zone 2, and $1 + (\theta - 2\pi + y)$ on
Zone 3. Zone 1 further splits by the phase of $\rho_3$ at time
$1 + \theta$:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{1a} ($\theta \in [0,\, y]$): $\rho_3$ is scanning on the
boundary at angle $(2\pi - y) + \theta \pmod{2\pi}$.
\item \textbf{1b} ($\theta \in [y,\, 1+y]$): $\rho_3$ is on its
boundary-to-origin transit, at position $(1 + y - \theta,\, 0)$.
\item \textbf{1c} ($\theta \in [1+y,\, L]$): $\rho_3$ is on its
origin-to-$C$ redeploy, at position $(\theta - 1 - y)\cdot e(L)$.
\end{itemize}
The problem is invariant under the reflection $\theta \mapsto (2\pi - y)
- \theta$ exchanging $\rho_1 \leftrightarrow \rho_2$; Zone 2 is the image
of Zone 1 under this reflection.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The finder-zone partition follows from \Cref{def:a3}: $\rho_1$'s scan
arc is $[0, L]$, $\rho_2$'s is $[L, 2\pi - y]$, $\rho_3$'s is $[2\pi -
y, 2\pi)$, and these tile $[0, 2\pi)$ because $2L + y = 2\pi$. The
discovery-time expressions follow since each scanning robot traverses
its arc at unit speed starting at time $1$. For the sub-zones: $\rho_3$
has phases $[0,1]$ (approach), $[1, 1+y]$ (scan), $[1+y,\, 2+y]$
(boundary-to-origin), $[2+y,\, 3+y]$ (origin-to-$C$), $[3+y, \infty)$
(parked). So for $\theta \in [0, L]$ with $1 + \theta \in [1,\, 1+L]$,
we have $1 + \theta \in [1, 1+y]$ iff $\theta \in [0, y]$ (sub-zone
1a); $1 + \theta \in [1+y, 2+y]$ iff $\theta \in [y, 1+y]$ (1b); $1 +
\theta \in [2+y, 3+y]$ iff $\theta \in [1+y, 2+y]$, which intersected
with $[0, L]$ gives $[1+y, L]$ (1c), using $L \le 2 + y$ (equivalent to
$y \ge 2(\pi-2)/3$). The explicit position formulas follow by plugging
the elapsed-phase time into each phase's trajectory. Reflection
invariance is immediate from the symmetry of \Cref{def:a3}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Zone-wise maxima at $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$}

For compactness write $c_i(\theta) := \lvert\rho_i(t_{\mathrm{find}}
(\theta)) - e(\theta)\rvert$ for $i \in \{1,2,3\}$, so that
$\mathrm{evac}(\theta) = t_{\mathrm{find}}(\theta) + \max_i c_i(\theta)$
by~\eqref{eq:evac}.

\begin{lemma}[Zone maxima at $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$]
\label{lem:zone-max}
Let $y_{\mathrm{opt}} \in \bigl(\tfrac{2(\pi-2)}{3},\,
\tfrac{4\pi}{9}\bigr)$ be the parameter defined in
\Cref{thm:transcendental}, and set $T_1 := T_1(y_{\mathrm{opt}})$, $T_3
:= T_3(y_{\mathrm{opt}})$.
\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Zone 1a.} $\mathrm{evac}(\theta) = 1 + \theta + 2\sin(y/2 +
\theta)$ on $[0, y]$ and is strictly increasing. Hence $\max_{\theta
\in [0, y]} \mathrm{evac}(\theta) = 1 + y + 2\sin(3y/2) \approx 4.1522
< T_1$ at $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}}$.
\item \emph{Zone 1b.} The map $\theta \mapsto 1 + \theta + c_2(\theta)$
attains an interior maximum of $T_1$ at $\theta = 2\pi/3 - y/2$,
where $c_2 = \sqrt 3 > c_3$. On the $c_3$-dominant sub-interval, $1 +
\theta + c_3(\theta)$ has no interior critical point, and its maximum
at the endpoint $\theta = 1 + y$ equals $3 + y_{\mathrm{opt}} < T_1$.
Consequently $\max_{\theta \in [y,\, 1+y]} \mathrm{evac}(\theta) = T_1$.
\item \emph{Zone 1c.} $c_3(\theta) > c_2(\theta)$ throughout $[1+y,\,
L]$. The function $\theta \mapsto 1 + \theta + c_3(\theta)$ has a
unique interior critical point at $\theta_3^*(y_{\mathrm{opt}})$, with
value $T_3 = T_1$. Both endpoints give $3 + y_{\mathrm{opt}} < T_1$.
\item \emph{Zone 3.} $c_1 = c_2 = 2\sin(y/2)$ is constant and $c_3 = 0$;
the maximum is $1 + y + 2\sin y \approx 4.0912 < T_1$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
\textbf{Zone 1a.} The finder $\rho_1$ is at the exit, so $c_1 = 0$. By
\Cref{lem:zones}(1a), $\rho_3$ is on the boundary at angle $(2\pi - y) +
\theta$, so $c_3 = 2\lvert\sin((2\pi - y)/2)\rvert = 2\sin(y/2)$
(independent of $\theta$). $\rho_2$ is still scanning at angle $(2\pi -
y) - \theta$, so as computed in the proof of \Cref{prop:case1},
$c_2 = 2\sin(y/2 + \theta)$. For $\theta \in (0, y]$ and $y < \pi/2$,
$\sin(y/2 + \theta) > \sin(y/2)$, so the bottleneck is $\rho_2$ and
$\mathrm{evac}(\theta) = 1 + \theta + 2\sin(y/2 + \theta)$. Its derivative
is $1 + 2\cos(y/2 + \theta)$; on $[0, y]$ the argument $y/2 + \theta$
ranges in $[y/2, 3y/2] \subset (0, \pi)$, and at $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}}
\approx 1.216$ we have $\cos(3y/2) \approx -0.25 > -1/2$, so the
derivative is positive throughout. Hence $\mathrm{evac}$ is strictly
increasing, attaining $1 + y + 2\sin(3y/2) \approx 4.152$ at the
right endpoint.

\textbf{Zone 1b.} Here $\rho_3(1+\theta) = (1 + y - \theta,\, 0)$ by
\Cref{lem:zones}(1b), so
\[
c_3(\theta)^2 = (1 + y - \theta - \cos\theta)^2 + \sin^2\theta
= (1+y-\theta)^2 - 2(1+y-\theta)\cos\theta + 1,
\]
and $c_2(\theta) = 2\sin(y/2 + \theta)$ as in Zone 1a. The interior
critical point of $\theta \mapsto 1 + \theta + c_2$ satisfies $1 +
2\cos(y/2 + \theta) = 0$, i.e., $y/2 + \theta = 2\pi/3$; at $y =
y_{\mathrm{opt}}$ this gives $\theta = 2\pi/3 - y/2 \approx 1.486$,
which lies in $[y, 1+y] \approx [1.22,\, 2.22]$. There $c_2 =
2\sin(2\pi/3) = \sqrt 3$, and a direct computation (at
$y_{\mathrm{opt}}$) gives $c_3 = \sqrt{(1 + y - \theta_1)^2 - 2(1 + y -
\theta_1)\cos\theta_1 + 1} \approx 1.188 < \sqrt 3$, confirming
$\rho_2$ is the bottleneck at this $\theta$. Thus the interior critical
value is $\mathrm{evac} = 1 + (2\pi/3 - y/2) + \sqrt 3 = T_1(y)$.

On the sub-interval where $c_3 > c_2$, the equation $\frac{d}{d\theta}(1
+ \theta + c_3) = 0$ reduces (after differentiation of the $c_3$-formula
and algebraic manipulation) to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:zone1b-crit}
c_3 + (1 + y - \theta)(1 - \sin\theta) - \cos\theta = 0,
\end{equation}
which has no solution in $[y, 1+y]$ at $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}}$; this is
verified symbolically in the Wolfram script
\texttt{analysis/a3\_zone\_proof.wls}, where
\textsf{NSolve} on~\eqref{eq:zone1b-crit} returns the empty set.
Consequently the $c_3$-branch maximum is at an endpoint: at $\theta = y$
one has $c_3 = 2\sin(y/2)$ and $\mathrm{evac} = 1 + y + 2\sin(y/2)$,
strictly less than the Zone 3 maximum $1 + y + 2\sin y$; at $\theta
= 1 + y$, $\rho_3$ is at the origin with $c_3 = 1$ and $\mathrm{evac} =
3 + y = T_2(y) < T_1$ at $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$ (since Case 2 is slack at
the balance point). Thus the Zone 1b maximum is $T_1$.

\textbf{Zone 1c.} $\rho_3(1+\theta) = (\theta - 1 - y) \cdot e(L)$ by
\Cref{lem:zones}(1c); writing $u := \theta - 1 - y$ and using $\langle
e(L), e(\theta) \rangle = \cos(L - \theta)$,
\[
c_3(\theta)^2 = u^2 - 2u\cos(L - \theta) + 1.
\]
On $[1+y,\, L]$, the argument $L - \theta$ lies in $[0,\, L - 1 - y]$,
and with $L - 1 - y = \pi - 3y/2 - 1 \approx 0.32$ at $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$,
we have $u \in [0,\, 0.32]$. Numerical evaluation at the critical point
$\theta_3^* \approx 2.331$ gives $c_3 \approx 0.888$ and $c_2 = 2\sin(y/2
+ \theta_3^*) \approx 0.403$; continuity and monotonicity arguments
(verified in \texttt{analysis/a3\_zone\_proof.wls}) extend this to $c_3
> c_2$ throughout $[1+y,\, L]$. Hence $\mathrm{evac} = 1 + \theta + c_3$
on this zone. Setting $v := L - \theta$ and $W := \pi - 3y/2 - 1$, the
critical-point condition $\frac{d}{d\theta}(1 + \theta + c_3) = 0$
reduces (see \Cref{thm:transcendental} for the derivation) to a
quadratic in $W - v$ whose admissible root and the balance $T_1 = T_3$
together characterise $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$. The critical value equals
$T_3 = T_1$ by construction. Both endpoint values equal $3 +
y_{\mathrm{opt}} < T_1$ (same argument as Zone 1b).

\textbf{Zone 3.} Set $u := \theta - (2\pi - y) \in [0, y]$, so
$t_{\mathrm{find}} = 1 + u$ and $\rho_3(1+u) = e(2\pi - y + u) =
e(\theta)$, giving $c_3 = 0$. $\rho_1$ is scanning on the boundary at
angle $u$, and $\rho_2$ at angle $(2\pi - y) - u$, so
\begin{align*}
c_1(u) &= 2\lvert\sin((u - \theta)/2)\rvert = 2\sin(y/2) \quad
\text{(constant)},\\
c_2(u) &= 2\lvert\sin(((2\pi - y - u) - \theta)/2)\rvert = 2\sin u.
\end{align*}
Thus $\mathrm{evac}(u) = 1 + u + \max(2\sin(y/2),\, 2\sin u)$. Since
$y < \pi$, $\sin u \le \sin(y/2)$ iff $u \le y/2$. On $u \in [0,
y/2]$ the $\max$ is $2\sin(y/2)$ and $\mathrm{evac}$ is strictly
increasing, attaining $1 + y/2 + 2\sin(y/2)$ at $u = y/2$. On $u \in
[y/2, y]$ the $\max$ is $2\sin u$, and $\frac{d}{du}(1 + u + 2\sin u) =
1 + 2\cos u > 0$ for $u \in [0, 2\pi/3]$, so $\mathrm{evac}$ is again
strictly increasing, attaining $1 + y + 2\sin y$ at $u = y$. This is
the larger of the two, and at $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}} \approx 1.216$ it
equals $\approx 4.0912 < T_1$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Main theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Improved upper bound]
\label{thm:main}
Let $y_{\mathrm{opt}} \in \bigl(\tfrac{2(\pi-2)}{3},\, \tfrac{4\pi}{9}\bigr)$
be the unique value characterised by \Cref{thm:transcendental}. The
algorithm $A_3(y_{\mathrm{opt}})$ has worst-case evacuation time
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:main}
T(y_{\mathrm{opt}}) \;=\; 1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y_{\mathrm{opt}}}{2}
+ \sqrt 3 \;\approx\; 4.21851699389745\ldots,
\end{equation}
attained at exactly four exit angles --- $\theta_1 = 2\pi/3 - y_{\mathrm{opt}}/2$,
$\theta_3^* = \pi - y_{\mathrm{opt}}/2 - v_{\mathrm{opt}}$, and their
reflections across $\pi$. This improves the Czyzowicz et
al.~\cite{czyzowicz2014} upper bound of $\approx 4.21930$ by
$\approx 0.00078 \approx 1/1280$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By~\eqref{eq:evac} the function $\mathrm{evac} : [0, 2\pi) \to \mathbb R$
is well-defined. By \Cref{lem:zones} it is piecewise-smooth with
breakpoints at the zone boundaries, and by \Cref{lem:zone-max} its
maximum on each zone of Zone~1 is either $T_1$ (attained at an interior
critical point in Zone 1b or Zone 1c) or strictly less than $T_1$. Zone
2 is the image of Zone 1 under the reflection of \Cref{lem:zones}, under
which $\mathrm{evac}$ is invariant, so the Zone 2 maximum also equals
$T_1$ and is attained at two reflected angles. Zone 3 is bounded by $1 +
y_{\mathrm{opt}} + 2\sin y_{\mathrm{opt}} \approx 4.0912 < T_1$. Hence
$T(y_{\mathrm{opt}}) = \max_\theta \mathrm{evac}(\theta) = T_1$, attained at
exactly the four angles listed. Equation~\eqref{eq:T1} gives the closed
form in~\eqref{eq:main}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Transcendental characterisation of $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$}
\label{sec:transcendental}

\begin{theorem}[Transcendental characterisation]
\label{thm:transcendental}
Fix $y \in \bigl(\tfrac{2(\pi-2)}{3},\, \tfrac{4\pi}{9}\bigr)$ and set
$W := \pi - 3y/2 - 1$. On Zone 1c, parametrise the exit as
$\theta = L - v$ with $v \in (0, W)$. The critical-point condition
$\tfrac{d}{dv} T_3(y, L - v) = 0$ is equivalent to the quadratic in
$W - v$
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:quadratic}
(W-v)^2 (2 - \sin v) - 2(W-v)\cos v + \sin v \;=\; 0,
\end{equation}
whose geometrically admissible (i.e.\ $0 \le W - v \le W$) root is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:admissible}
W - v \;=\; \frac{\cos v - \sqrt{1 - 2\sin v}}{2 - \sin v}.
\end{equation}
Combining~\eqref{eq:admissible} with $T_1(y) = T_3(y, L - v)$ yields the
\emph{master equation}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:master}
\frac{\cos v + (1 - \sin v)\sqrt{1 - 2\sin v}}{2 - \sin v}
\;=\; v + \sqrt 3 - \tfrac{\pi}{3}.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:master} has a unique root $v_{\mathrm{opt}} \in (0,
\pi/6)$. Setting
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:yopt}
y_{\mathrm{opt}} \;:=\;
\tfrac{2}{3}\!\left(\pi - 1 - v_{\mathrm{opt}}
  - \tfrac{\cos v_{\mathrm{opt}} - \sqrt{1 - 2\sin v_{\mathrm{opt}}}}
         {2 - \sin v_{\mathrm{opt}}}\right),
\end{equation}
the worst-case evacuation time of $A_3(y_{\mathrm{opt}})$
satisfies~\eqref{eq:main}, which also equals
$\tfrac{4}{3} + \tfrac{\pi}{3} + \sqrt 3 + \tfrac{W_{\mathrm{opt}}}{3}$ for
$W_{\mathrm{opt}} := \pi - 3 y_{\mathrm{opt}}/2 - 1$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \Cref{lem:zones}(1c), $\rho_3(1 + \theta) = (\theta - 1 - y)\cdot
e(L)$ on Zone 1c. Writing $u := \theta - 1 - y = W - v$ (via $\theta =
L - v$),
\[
c_3^2 \;=\; u^2 - 2u\langle e(L), e(\theta)\rangle + 1
\;=\; (W-v)^2 - 2(W - v)\cos v + 1,
\]
using $\langle e(L), e(L - v)\rangle = \cos v$. Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:T3-param}
T_3(y, L - v) \;=\; 1 + (L - v) + c_3,
\end{equation}
and $\tfrac{d}{d\theta}(\cdot) = -\tfrac{d}{dv}(\cdot)$, so the
critical point condition $\tfrac{d}{d\theta} T_3 = 0$ becomes
$\tfrac{d c_3}{d v} = 1$. Differentiating $c_3^2$ in $v$ gives
\[
2 c_3 \tfrac{d c_3}{d v}
\;=\; -2(W-v) + 2\cos v + 2(W-v)\sin v
\;=\; 2\bigl[\cos v - (W-v)(1 - \sin v)\bigr],
\]
so $\tfrac{d c_3}{dv} = 1$ is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:c3-crit}
c_3 \;=\; \cos v - (W - v)(1 - \sin v).
\end{equation}
Squaring~\eqref{eq:c3-crit} and equating to the formula for $c_3^2$:
\begin{align*}
&(W-v)^2 (1 - \sin v)^2 - 2(W-v)(1 - \sin v)\cos v + \cos^2 v \\
&\qquad = (W-v)^2 - 2(W-v)\cos v + 1,
\end{align*}
which simplifies (using $(1-\sin v)^2 - 1 = -2\sin v + \sin^2 v$ and
$\cos^2 v - 1 = -\sin^2 v$) to
\[
-(W-v)^2 \sin v(2 - \sin v) + 2(W-v)\cos v \sin v - \sin^2 v \;=\; 0.
\]
Dividing by $-\sin v > 0$ yields~\eqref{eq:quadratic}.

Solving~\eqref{eq:quadratic} as a quadratic in $W-v$:
\[
W - v \;=\; \frac{\cos v \pm \sqrt{\cos^2 v - (2-\sin v)\sin v}}{2 - \sin v}
\;=\; \frac{\cos v \pm \sqrt{1 - 2\sin v}}{2 - \sin v},
\]
using $\cos^2 v - 2\sin v + \sin^2 v = 1 - 2\sin v$. For $v \in (0,
\pi/6)$, $1 - 2\sin v > 0$ so the two roots are real and distinct. Only
the $-$ branch satisfies $W - v \le W$: at $v \to 0^+$, the $-$ branch
tends to $0$ while the $+$ branch tends to $\tfrac{1 + 1}{2} = 1 > W$
(with $W \approx 0.32$ at $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$). This establishes~\eqref{eq:admissible}.

Substituting~\eqref{eq:admissible} into~\eqref{eq:c3-crit}:
\begin{align*}
c_3
&\;=\;\cos v - \tfrac{(\cos v - \sqrt{1-2\sin v})(1 - \sin v)}{2 - \sin v} \\
&\;=\; \tfrac{\cos v(2 - \sin v) - (\cos v - \sqrt{1-2\sin v})(1 - \sin v)}{2 - \sin v} \\
&\;=\; \tfrac{\cos v + (1 - \sin v)\sqrt{1 - 2\sin v}}{2 - \sin v},
\end{align*}
since $\cos v(2 - \sin v) - \cos v(1 - \sin v) = \cos v$. The balance
$T_1(y) = T_3(y, L - v)$ reads
\[
1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y}{2} + \sqrt 3
\;=\; 1 + L - v + c_3
\;=\; 1 + \pi - \tfrac{y}{2} - v + c_3,
\]
equivalent to $c_3 = v + \sqrt 3 - \tfrac{\pi}{3}$. Equating the two
expressions for $c_3$ gives~\eqref{eq:master}.

\emph{Uniqueness of $v_{\mathrm{opt}} \in (0, \pi/6)$.}
Let $f(v) := \tfrac{\cos v + (1 - \sin v)\sqrt{1 - 2\sin v}}{2 - \sin v}$
and $g(v) := v + \sqrt 3 - \pi/3$. Clearly $g'(v) = 1 > 0$. A direct
computation (carried out in \texttt{analysis/a3\_balance.wls} and
verified at 30-digit precision) gives $f'(v) < 0$ for all $v \in
(0, \pi/6)$. At $v = 0^+$, $f(0) = \tfrac{1 + 1\cdot 1}{2} = 1$ and
$g(0) = \sqrt 3 - \pi/3 \approx 0.685$, so $f - g > 0$. At $v = \pi/6$,
$1 - 2\sin v = 0$, so $f(\pi/6) = \tfrac{\cos(\pi/6)}{2 - 1/2} =
\tfrac{\sqrt 3/2}{3/2} = \tfrac{\sqrt 3}{3} \approx 0.577$, while
$g(\pi/6) \approx 0.685 + 0.524 \approx 1.209$, so $f - g < 0$. Since
$f - g$ is continuous and strictly decreasing (sum of a decreasing
function and $-g$), it has a unique zero in $(0, \pi/6)$.

Finally, from $W = \pi - 3y/2 - 1$, one has $y = \tfrac{2}{3}(\pi - 1 -
W)$; substituting $W = v + (W - v)$ with~\eqref{eq:admissible}
gives~\eqref{eq:yopt}. And $T_1 = 1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y}{2} +
\sqrt 3 = \tfrac{4}{3} + \tfrac{\pi}{3} + \sqrt 3 + \tfrac{W}{3}$ follows
by direct algebra. This establishes~\eqref{eq:main}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[On ``no elementary closed form'']
\label{rem:pslq}
An integer-relation search (PSLQ as implemented by Mathematica's
\texttt{FindIntegerNullVector}) at $40$-digit precision, on the basis
$\{1,\, \pi,\, \sqrt 3,\, \sqrt 2,\, \log 2,\, \log 3,\, \gamma\}$,
finds no $\mathbb Z$-linear relation with $v_{\mathrm{opt}}$ whose
coefficient vector has $\ell^\infty$-norm at most $20$; the algorithm
certifies non-existence of such a relation of norm at most $\approx
15.2$. The same holds for $T(y_{\mathrm{opt}})$. This rules out, for
these specific atomic constants and norm thresholds, any closed-form
expression of the form $\sum_j a_j b_j$ with integer coefficients
$a_j$. We make no claim about transcendence of $v_{\mathrm{opt}}$ in the
Liouville sense; we observe only the absence of a \emph{short}
$\mathbb Z$-linear reduction over the standard atomic basis. See
\texttt{analysis/a3\_balance.wls}.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[High-precision numerical values]
\label{cor:hp}
To $40$ decimal digits, \eqref{eq:master} and~\eqref{eq:yopt} yield
\[
\begin{aligned}
v_{\mathrm{opt}} &= 0.20283\,77447\,40492\,87031\,05459\,53643\,95496\,52206,\\
y_{\mathrm{opt}} &= 1.21585\,78321\,29242\,90943\,42098\,32081\,15330\,17778,\\
T(y_{\mathrm{opt}}) &= 4.21851\,69938\,97451\,33111\,87703\,47651\,63097\,21854.
\end{aligned}
\]
\end{corollary}

\section{Numerical verification}
\label{sec:experiments}

The proofs in~\Cref{sec:main} are closed-form modulo the single
transcendental root $v_{\mathrm{opt}}$ of~\eqref{eq:master}, but several
steps (non-existence of interior critical points on sub-intervals,
sign verifications, uniqueness arguments) rely on numerical evaluation
at specified precision. We accompany the paper with reproducible
scripts that check each such step.

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{analysis/a3\_balance.wls} (Wolfram Language): solves
\eqref{eq:master} numerically at 50-digit precision and verifies the
monotonicity of both sides on $(0, \pi/6)$. Also performs the
integer-relation search underlying \Cref{rem:pslq}.
\item \texttt{analysis/a3\_zone\_proof.wls}: verifies the claims of
\Cref{lem:zone-max} zone-by-zone at $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}}$, including
the absence of interior critical points of $1 + \theta + c_3$ in the
$c_3$-dominant subregion of Zone 1b.
\item \texttt{experiments/wireless\_k3.py}, \texttt{experiments/a3\_verify.py}
(Python): a general-purpose $k$-robot wireless evacuation simulator;
\texttt{a3\_verify.py} confirms the simulator reproduces $T_{\mathrm{CGK}} =
\frac{4\pi}{9} + \frac{2\sqrt 3 + 5}{3} + \frac{1}{600}$ at the

providing an independent check on the simulator's accuracy.
\item \texttt{experiments/a3\_optimize.py}: scalar search for

\item \texttt{experiments/a3\_local\_search.py}: Nelder--Mead over a

does not help.
\item \texttt{experiments/a3\_diagnose.py}: identifies the four

\end{itemize}

\section{Improved lower bound}
\label{sec:lb}

We prove \Cref{thm:lb-strict} below: $T^*_{3,\mathrm{wireless}} >
4.15937$ strictly, improving \eqref{eq:czy-lb}, and $T^*_{3,\mathrm{
wireless}} \ge 4.17306$ for algorithms that saturate the perimeter scan
budget at the critical time $1 + x^*$ (the \emph{scan-tight} subclass;
see below). The argument has three ingredients:
\Cref{prop:refined} (a new universal inequality bounding evacuation time
by the bottleneck-robot chord), \Cref{lem:state-cgk} (a state-tight
sharpening of Czyzowicz et al.'s argument), and \Cref{lem:min-chord}
(a geometric min-chord lemma at scan-tight states). We conclude with
a discussion of the infimum over all algorithms
(\Cref{rem:infimum-open}), which numerics locate at approximately
$4.16703$.

\paragraph{Where Czyzowicz et al.'s argument has slack.} Czyzowicz et
al.'s proof of~\eqref{eq:czy-lb} bounds the \emph{finder}'s chord to the
exit. In $A_3(y_{\mathrm{opt}})$ at its worst exit $\theta \approx
1.486$, the finder is at the exit (chord $0$) and the bottleneck is
the robot diametrically across the scanned arc, with chord $\sqrt 3
\approx 1.732$ --- strictly larger than Czyzowicz et al.'s finder-chord
lower bound $2\sin(xk/2) \approx 1.586$ evaluated at the same $x$. This
discrepancy motivates a bound on the \emph{bottleneck} chord, which
is what \Cref{prop:refined} provides.

\begin{proposition}[Refined inequality]
\label{prop:refined}
For any $3$-robot wireless algorithm, any $x > 0$, and any boundary
point $p$ unexplored at time $1+x$,
\[
\mathrm{evac}(p) \;\ge\; (1+x) + \max_{j \in \{1,2,3\}} \bigl\|\rho_j(1+x) - p\bigr\|.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $t_p \ge 1 + x$ be the first-visit time of $p$. At $t_p$, the finder
is at $p$; every robot $j$ has travelled at most $t_p - (1+x)$ from its
position at time $1+x$, so by the triangle inequality
\[
\|\rho_j(t_p) - p\| \;\ge\; \|\rho_j(1+x) - p\| - \bigl(t_p - (1+x)\bigr).
\]
Hence $\max_j \|\rho_j(t_p) - p\| \ge \max_j \|\rho_j(1+x) - p\| - (t_p - (1+x))$,
and $\mathrm{evac}(p) = t_p + \max_j \|\rho_j(t_p) - p\|$ gives the stated bound
when the RHS is $\ge 0$. Otherwise $t_p - (1+x) > \max_j \|\rho_j(1+x) - p\|$,
and $\mathrm{evac}(p) \ge t_p \ge (1+x) + \max_j \|\rho_j(1+x) - p\|$ still.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[State-tight CGK]
\label{lem:state-cgk}
For any $3$-robot algorithm and any $x > 0$, letting $e := |E(1+x)|
\le 3x$ be the measure of the set of boundary points visited by time $1+x$,
\[
T(\mathcal A) \;\ge\; \begin{cases}
1 + x + 2\sin\bigl((2\pi - e)/2\bigr) & \text{if } e \ge \pi, \\
1 + x + 2 & \text{if } e < \pi.
\end{cases}
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Lemma~5 of~\cite{czyzowicz2014} gives two unexplored points at
shorter-arc distance $\ge \min(2\pi - e, \pi)$; the chord is $2\sin$ of
half of this. Applying Lemma~6 of~\cite{czyzowicz2014} to these two
points yields the stated bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Min-chord geometric lemma]
\label{lem:min-chord}
For any $3$ robots $R_1, R_2, R_3$ on the unit circle and any $3$
disjoint boundary arcs of total length $3x$ (with $x \in (\pi/3,
2\pi/3)$) ending at these robot positions,
\[
\sup_{p \in E^c} \max_{j \in \{1,2,3\}} \|R_j - p\|
\;\ge\; 2\cos(x/4).
\]
The bound is tight, attained by the ``2-coincident'' configuration with
$R_1 = e(\pi + x/2)$, $R_2 = R_3 = e(0)$, scan arcs $[\pi - x/2, \pi +
x/2]$, $[0, x]$, $[-x, 0]$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
\emph{Tightness.} Place $R_1 = e(d)$ with $d = \pi + x/2$ and $R_2 =
R_3 = e(0)$, scans as described. The explored set is $[-x, x] \cup [d -
x, d] = [-x, x] \cup [\pi - x/2, \pi + x/2]$ (disjoint for $x < 2\pi/3$).
Unexplored is $(x, d - x) \cup (d, 2\pi - x)$. A direct computation (with
$\sin(\pi/2 - x/4) = \cos(x/4)$ and $\sin(\pi/2 + x/4) = \cos(x/4)$
showing both arc sups coincide) gives the sup chord exactly $2\cos(x/4)$;
this is verified symbolically in \texttt{analysis/lb\_theorem.wls}.

\emph{Lower bound.} Given any configuration, if all three robots are
distinct on the circle, we may translate two of them towards each other
along the circle (while sliding their arcs to preserve $|E| = 3x$)
without increasing $\sup_p \max_j \|R_j - p\|$: since $\max_j$ is taken
over three arguments, merging two of them weakly decreases the max.
Hence the infimum of the sup chord over all configurations equals that
over the $2$-coincident subfamily, which by direct optimisation over the
separation $d$ equals $2\cos(x/4)$, attained at $d = \pi + x/2$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Strict LB improvement]
\label{thm:lb-strict}
For any valid $3$-robot wireless evacuation algorithm $\mathcal A$,
\[
T(\mathcal A) \;>\; 1 + x^* + 2\sin(3x^*/2) \;=\; 4.15937\ldots.
\]
In particular, any algorithm $\mathcal A$ satisfying the ``scan-tight''
condition $|E(1 + x^*)| = 3x^*$ at time $1 + x^*$ satisfies
\[
T(\mathcal A) \;\ge\; 1 + x^* + 2\cos(x^*/4) \;=\; 4.17306\ldots
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Fix any valid algorithm $\mathcal A$ and consider its state at time
$1 + x^*$. Let $e := |E(1 + x^*)| \le 3x^*$.

\emph{Case 1: $e < 3x^*$.} Since $e \ge \pi$ or $e < \pi$,
Lemma~\ref{lem:state-cgk} gives $T(\mathcal A) \ge 1 + x^* + 2\sin
((2\pi - e)/2)$ (for $e \ge \pi$) or $T(\mathcal A) \ge 1 + x^* + 2$
(for $e < \pi$). On $[\pi, 3x^*]$, the function $e \mapsto 2\sin((2\pi
- e)/2)$ is strictly decreasing in $e$ (as $(2\pi - e)/2 \in (0, \pi/2)$
is strictly decreasing in $e$ and $\sin$ is strictly increasing on
$(0, \pi/2)$), so $e < 3x^*$ gives strictly $T(\mathcal A) > 1 + x^* +
2\sin(3x^*/2) = 4.15937$.

\emph{Case 2: $e = 3x^*$.} The scan budget is saturated, so all three
robots are on $\partial D$ at time $1 + x^*$ (each has been continuously
scanning since time $1$). By Lemma~\ref{lem:min-chord},
$\sup_{p \in E^c} \max_j \|R_j - p\| \ge 2\cos(x^*/4)$; by
Proposition~\ref{prop:refined}, $T(\mathcal A) \ge 1 + x^* + 2\cos(x^*/4)
= 4.17306 > 4.15937$.

In either case, $T(\mathcal A) > 4.15937$, and in Case 2 the stronger
bound $4.17306$ holds.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[On the infimum]
\label{rem:infimum-open}
\Cref{thm:lb-strict} shows $T(\mathcal A) > 4.15937$ strictly for every
specific algorithm $\mathcal A$, but its proof leaves open the
quantitative value of $\inf_{\mathcal A} T(\mathcal A)$: in Case~1 of
the proof, as $e \to 3x^{*-}$ the Case~1 bound approaches $4.15937$, so
the proof as written does not exclude $\inf_{\mathcal A} T(\mathcal A) =
4.15937$.

A uniform quantitative improvement comes from analysing how much the
refined bound can drop below its scan-tight value $1 + x^* + 2\cos(x^*/4)
\approx 4.17306$ as the scan budget slackens. Consider the ``balanced
slack'' subfamily: each of the three robots uses the same time
$\tau \in [0, x^*]$ for radial inward motion after its scan arc, so
that $|E| = 3(x^* - \tau) = 3x^* - 3\tau$ and each $R_j = (1-\tau)e
(\alpha_j + s_j L_j)$ with $L_j = x^* - \tau$. Numerically (see
\texttt{experiments/lb\_uniform.py} and
\texttt{analysis/lb\_uniform\_bound.wls}), the combined bound
$\max(\text{\Cref{lem:state-cgk}},\text{\Cref{prop:refined}})$ on this
subfamily has its minimum at
$\tau^* \approx 0.007782$, where the state-tight CGK rate (rising at
slope $\approx 1/3$ as $\tau$ grows from $0$) crosses the refined rate
(falling at slope $\approx 0.26$), giving the common value
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:inf-bal}
T^*_{3,\mathrm{wireless}} \;\ge\; \min_{\tau \ge 0} \max\!\bigl(
\mathrm{CGK}(3x^* - 3\tau),\, 1 + x^* + h(\tau)\bigr)
\;\approx\; 4.16703\ldots,
\end{equation}
where $h(\tau) := \sqrt{(1-\tau)^2 + 2(1-\tau)\cos((x^*-\tau)/2) + 1}$
is the sup chord in the balanced 2-coincident configuration.

For the bound \eqref{eq:inf-bal} to become a rigorous universal
lower bound, one needs to extend \Cref{lem:min-chord} to all algorithm
states with $|E| \le 3x^*$ (possibly with off-boundary robots), showing
that no configuration achieves a smaller sup chord than the balanced
2-coincident benchmark. Broad numerical minimax probes (see
\texttt{experiments/lb\_uniform\_broad.py}) are consistent with this,
modulo grid artefacts; a rigorous proof is open.
\end{remark}

\section{Next Steps}
\label{sec:next}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Richer algorithm class.} We numerically explored three
natural enrichments and found none that beat \Cref{thm:main}:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Family C} (\texttt{experiments/family\_c.py}): $r_3$ also
scans (with length $L_b > 0$) after redeployment. Local search from the
$A_3$ optimum drives $L_b \to 0$; the second scan begins at time $3 + y
> 4.21$, after the worst-case evacuation, so it cannot contribute to
coverage in the binding regime.
\item \textbf{Family D} (\texttt{experiments/family\_d.py}): fully
$3$-fold symmetric, all three robots perform return-to-origin +
redeploy. This caps at the naive bound $1 + 2\pi/3 + \sqrt 3 \approx
4.826$. The asymmetry of $A_3$ — a \emph{single} robot acting as
"backup" at the long-arc midpoint — is structural, not cosmetic.
\item \textbf{Family E} (\texttt{experiments/family\_e.py}): both $r_2$
and $r_3$ redeploy while $r_1$ remains simple. All local searches
return to the $A_3$ optimum.
\end{itemize}
We conjecture that $4.21852$ is a local minimum across piecewise-linear
plus boundary-scan plus chord trajectory families at $k=3$. Breaking
through likely requires \emph{curved} (non-radial) approaches, which
would admit a larger design space at the cost of harder analysis.
\item \textbf{Lower bound (infimum).} By
\Cref{rem:infimum-open} and \eqref{eq:inf-bal}, the conjectural
universal lower bound is $\approx 4.16703$, attained in the balanced
2-coincident subfamily at $\tau^* \approx 0.007782$. Closing this
rigorously reduces to extending \Cref{lem:min-chord} to all
non-scan-tight states: for each $|E| \in (0, 3x^*]$, show that no
algorithm state achieves a refined sup chord smaller than the balanced
2-coincident benchmark $h(\tau)$ with $\tau = (3x^* - |E|)/3$. Broad
numerical minimax (see \texttt{experiments/lb\_uniform\_broad.py})
supports this once grid artefacts are controlled.
\item \textbf{Closing the full gap.} Even the conjectural universal
LB of $4.17306$ leaves $\approx 0.045$ against our UB $4.21852$. A
multi-exit adversary keeping three mutually-far candidate exits alive
simultaneously is a natural next probe: by pigeonhole, some robot
must be far from whichever exit is committed to, regardless of
positioning. Formalising this for $k=3$ is open.
\end{itemize}
