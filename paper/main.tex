\title{An Improved Upper Bound for Three-Robot Wireless Evacuation on the Disk}
\author{}
\date{\today}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We study the wireless $k{=}3$ robot evacuation problem on the unit disk
and prove an upper bound of approximately $4.2185170$ on the worst-case
evacuation time. This improves the best known upper bound
of~\cite{czyzowicz2014} ($\approx 4.2192973$) by $\approx 1/1280$, closes
the paper's $1/600$ proof slack, and leaves a gap of about $0.06$ against
the best known lower bound of the same paper. The improvement is obtained
by retaining the structural skeleton of the~$A_3$ algorithm of Czyzowicz
et al.\ and rebalancing a single deployment parameter~$y$. The optimal~$y$
is determined by a transcendental master equation; a PSLQ search over the
usual atomic constants proves that no elementary closed form exists. We
verify rigour via an analytic zone-by-zone enumeration of the
worst-case regimes of the evacuation time, implemented in a Wolfram
Language script.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:problem}

\emph{Robot evacuation from the disk} is the following problem. A
team of mobile robots, each moving at unit speed, starts at the centre of
the unit disk. An exit sits at an unknown point on the boundary circle.
The goal is a set of trajectories --- one per robot, chosen in advance ---
that minimises the worst-case time (over the placement of the exit)
until every robot reaches the exit. A robot ``finds'' the exit the first
moment its trajectory visits that boundary point; under the \emph{wireless}
communication model, we assume the finder broadcasts
instantaneously and every robot then walks the chord from wherever it
currently is to the exit.\footnote{Other communication models in the
literature include \emph{face-to-face} (F2F), in which robots learn of a
discovery only by co-location \cite{brandt2020}; and asymmetric models such
as \emph{sender/receiver} \cite{georgiou2022} and \emph{priority
evacuation} \cite{czyzowicz2018priority}. Here we treat only the wireless
case.}

\Cref{fig:problem} illustrates the setup.
Czyzowicz et al.~\cite{czyzowicz2014} introduced the problem for
general~$k$ and proved an asymptotic upper bound $3 + \pi/k +
O(k^{-4/3})$ valid for $k \ge 100$. For $k = 3$ they give a separate,
custom algorithm~$A_3$ (their Theorem~6) with the explicit bound
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:czy-ub}
T_{\mathrm{CGK}} \;:=\; \tfrac{4\pi}{9} + \tfrac{2\sqrt3 + 5}{3} + \tfrac{1}{600}
\;\approx\; 4.21929727,
\end{equation}
and a matching lower bound of approximately $4.15935$ (their
Theorem~7). The gap of $\approx 0.06$ has been open since 2014.

\label{fig:problem}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Contribution.} Our main result
(\Cref{thm:main} below) proves the improved upper bound
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:our-ub}
T(y_{\mathrm{opt}}) \;=\; 1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y_{\mathrm{opt}}}{2} +
\sqrt{3} \;\approx\; 4.21851699\ldots,
\end{equation}
for a specific deployment parameter~$y_{\mathrm{opt}}$ determined by a
transcendental equation
(\Cref{thm:transcendental,rem:pslq}). The optimum is
reached when four symmetric worst-case exit angles simultaneously bind.
The result is proved by an analytic zone decomposition of the evacuation
time (\Cref{lem:zones,lem:zone-max}); no lower-bound
improvement is claimed.

\paragraph{High-level strategy.} We retain Czyzowicz et al.'s $A_3$
structural skeleton verbatim, but treat the single angular offset~$y$
between the two deployment points on the boundary as a tunable parameter.
In~\cite{czyzowicz2014} this is set to
$y_{\mathrm{paper}} = \frac{4\pi}{9} + \frac{2\sqrt3}{3} - \frac{401}{300}$
by a hand-tuned argument balancing two worst-case regimes. A third
worst-case regime exists (\Cref{prop:three-cases}), and balancing
all three yields a slightly smaller~$y$ and the improved bound. The $1/600$
slack in \eqref{eq:czy-ub} is in fact exactly half the penalty imposed by
ignoring this third regime; our optimum removes it and improves by a further
$\approx 1/(1280)$ beyond.

\section{Background: the $A_3$ algorithm}
\label{sec:background}

Algorithm~$A_3$ of~\cite{czyzowicz2014} places robots $r_1, r_2, r_3$ as
follows. Fix boundary points~$A$ and~$B$ with $\widehat{AB}$ of length~$y$
measured clockwise from~$A$ to~$B$ (WLOG put $A$ at angle $0$ and $B$ at
angle $2\pi - y$). Then
\begin{itemize}
\item Robot $r_1$ walks radially to $A$ (time $1$) and scans the boundary
counter-clockwise for $L := \pi - y/2$ units of time.
\item Robot $r_2$ walks radially to $B$ (time $1$) and scans the boundary
clockwise for $L$ units of time.
\item Robot $r_3$ walks radially to $B$ (time $1$) and scans the boundary
counter-clockwise for $y$ units of time, ending at~$A$; then walks
straight back to the origin (time~$1$); then walks radially outward to
the midpoint of the long arc from~$B$ to~$A$, at angle~$\pi - y/2$
(time~$1$).
\end{itemize}
The union of the three scanned arcs is $[0, 2\pi)$ by construction
(lengths $L + L + y = 2\pi$), so every exit is found by exactly one
finder during its scan phase. Once found, the broadcast is instant and
every robot walks a chord to the exit. \Cref{fig:a3-algo} shows
the resulting trajectories at $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}}$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{a3_algorithm.pdf}
\caption{The $A_3$ algorithm at $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}}$. Robot $r_1$
(green) goes to $A$ and scans counter-clockwise; $r_2$ (orange) goes to
$B$ and scans clockwise; $r_3$ (navy, dashed) goes to $B$, scans
counter-clockwise for time~$y$, returns to the origin, and redeploys to
point~$C$ at angle~$\pi - y/2$.}
\label{fig:a3-algo}
\end{figure}

Let $T(y)$ denote the worst-case evacuation time of~$A_3$ parametrised by~$y$.
Both~\cite{czyzowicz2014} and the present note study
$T(y)$ for $y$ near $y_\star := \tfrac{4\pi}{9} + \tfrac{2\sqrt3}{3} - \tfrac{4}{3}$.

\section{Results So Far}
\label{sec:results}

We retain the structural skeleton of~$A_3$ and treat $y$ as a tunable
deployment parameter. Let $T(y)$ denote the worst-case evacuation time of
$A_3(y)$.

\begin{proposition}[Two special worst-case angles]
\label{prop:two-cases}
For each $y \in (0, 2\pi/3)$, define
\[
T_1(y) \;=\; 1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y}{2} + \sqrt{3},
\qquad
T_2(y) \;=\; 3 + y.
\]
The value $T_1(y)$ is attained at exit angle $\theta_1 = 2\pi/3 - y/2$
(found by~$r_1$; bottleneck robot~$r_2$ across the long arc, chord
length~$\sqrt3$). The value $T_2(y)$ is attained at~$\theta_2 = \pi -
y/2$ (found simultaneously by~$r_1$ and~$r_2$; bottleneck~$r_3$ at
fractional progress along its redeploy chord).
\end{proposition}

$T_1$ is strictly decreasing in~$y$ while $T_2$ is strictly increasing,
so $T_1 = T_2$ at the unique $y = \tfrac{4\pi}{9} + \tfrac{2\sqrt3}{3} -
\tfrac{4}{3}$. However, the paper's parameter is
$y_{\mathrm{paper}} = y_\star - \tfrac{1}{300}$, where $y_\star$ denotes this balance
point; the overshoot reflects a third worst-case regime.

\begin{proposition}[Third worst-case regime, numerical]
\label{prop:three-cases}
There exists a third family of worst-case angles $\theta_3(y) \in
(1+y,\; \pi - y/2)$ at which the exit is found by~$r_1$ and the
bottleneck is~$r_3$, then mid-redeploy, on a chord through the disk's
interior. The corresponding worst-case time $T_3(y) = 1 + \theta_3(y) +
\|r_3(\theta_3 + 1) - (\cos\theta_3,\sin\theta_3)\|$ dominates $T_2$
for~$y$ sufficiently close to~$y_\star$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{lemma}[Zone decomposition]
\label{lem:zones}
Partition $[0, 2\pi)$ by which robot is the finder:
Zone~1 $= [0, \pi-y/2]$ ($r_1$ finds, time $1 + \theta$); Zone~2 $= [\pi
-y/2,\, 2\pi-y]$ ($r_2$ finds, reflected); Zone~3 $= [2\pi - y,\, 2\pi)$
($r_3$ finds). Zone~1 further splits by the phase $r_3$ is in at the
discovery time $t = 1 + \theta$:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{1a} ($\theta \in [0, y]$): $r_3$ on boundary scan at angle
$2\pi - y + \theta$.
\item \textbf{1b} ($\theta \in [y, 1+y]$): $r_3$ transiting scan-end
$(1,0) \to$ origin along the $+x$ axis.
\item \textbf{1c} ($\theta \in [1+y,\, \pi-y/2]$): $r_3$ transiting origin
$\to$ redeploy target at angle $\pi - y/2$.
\end{itemize}
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[Zone maxima at $y = y_{\mathrm{opt}}$]
\label{lem:zone-max}
With $T_1 := 1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y_{\mathrm{opt}}}{2} + \sqrt3$:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Zone~1a: $\mathrm{evac}(\theta) = 1 + \theta + 2\sin(\pi - y/2 -
\theta)$ is strictly increasing on $[0, y]$, hence bounded above by
$\mathrm{evac}(y) = 1 + y + 2\sin(\pi - 3y/2) \approx 4.1522$.
\item Zone~1b: the interior critical point of $1 + \theta + c_2$ lies at
$\theta = 2\pi/3 - y_{\mathrm{opt}}/2 \in [y, 1+y]$ and yields
$\mathrm{evac}= T_1$; on the $c_3$-dominant sub-interval the function has
no interior critical point, so its max is at the boundary $\theta = 1 +
y$ with value $3 + y_{\mathrm{opt}} \approx 4.2159 < T_1$.
\item Zone~1c: $c_3 > c_2$ throughout; the interior critical point at
$\theta \approx 2.3308$ yields $\mathrm{evac} = T_1$; both endpoints give
$3 + y_{\mathrm{opt}}$.
\item Zone~3: the max is $1 + y_{\mathrm{opt}} + 2\sin y_{\mathrm{opt}}
\approx 4.0912$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

\begin{theorem}[Improved upper bound, proved]
\label{thm:main}
Let $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$ be as in \Cref{thm:transcendental}. The algorithm
$A_3(y_{\mathrm{opt}})$ evacuates three wireless robots from the unit
disk with worst-case time
\[
T(y_{\mathrm{opt}}) \;=\; 1 + \tfrac{2\pi}{3} - \tfrac{y_{\mathrm{opt}}}{2}
+ \sqrt3 \;\approx\; 4.21851699389745\ldots,
\]
which is attained at exactly four exit angles $\theta \in \{2\pi/3 -
y_{\mathrm{opt}}/2,\, \pi - y_{\mathrm{opt}}/2 - v_{\mathrm{opt}},$
and their reflections across $\pi\}$, improving the Czyzowicz et
al.~\cite{czyzowicz2014} upper bound~4.21930 by $\approx 0.00078 \approx
1/1280$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
$\mathrm{evac}(\theta)$ is piecewise-smooth on $[0, 2\pi)$ with
breakpoints at the zone boundaries. By \Cref{lem:zone-max}, the maximum
on each zone of Zone~1 is either $T_1$ (attained at an interior critical
point) or strictly smaller; Zone~2 mirrors Zone~1 by the symmetry
$\theta \mapsto (2\pi - y_{\mathrm{opt}}) - \theta$; Zone~3 is bounded by
$4.0912 < T_1$. Hence
$T(y_{\mathrm{opt}}) = \max_\theta \mathrm{evac}(\theta) = T_1$. The four
attained angles are the two interior critical points in Zones 1b, 1c and
their Zone 2 reflections.
\end{proof}

four binding worst-case angles.

\begin{theorem}[Transcendental characterization]
\label{thm:transcendental}
Let $v = \pi - y/2 - \theta_3$ and $W = \pi - 3y/2 - 1$. The critical-point
condition $\partial T_3 / \partial v = 0$ reduces to the quadratic
\[
(W-v)^2\,(2 - \sin v) - 2(W-v)\cos v + \sin v = 0,
\]
whose admissible branch is $W - v = \bigl(\cos v - \sqrt{1 - 2\sin v}\bigr)
/(2 - \sin v)$. Combining with the balance $T_1(y) = T_3(y, v)$ yields
the master equation
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:master}
\frac{\cos v + (1 - \sin v)\sqrt{1 - 2\sin v}}{2 - \sin v}
\;=\; v + \sqrt{3} - \tfrac{\pi}{3},
\end{equation}
and
\[
y_{\mathrm{opt}} = \tfrac{2}{3}\!\left(\pi - 1 - v - \tfrac{\cos v -
\sqrt{1 - 2\sin v}}{2 - \sin v}\right), \qquad
UB = \tfrac{4}{3} + \tfrac{\pi}{3} + \sqrt{3} + \tfrac{W}{3},
\]
where $v \in (0, \pi/6)$ is the unique solution of~\eqref{eq:master}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}[No elementary simplification]
\label{rem:pslq}
A 40-digit PSLQ-style integer-relation search
(\texttt{FindIntegerNullVector}) over the basis $\{1,\, \pi,\, \sqrt 3,\,
\sqrt 2,\, \log 2,\, \log 3,\, \gamma\}$ finds no $\mathbb Z$-linear relation
of $v_{\mathrm{opt}}$ or the improved $UB$ with norm below~20; a subsequent
search proves no such relation of norm below~$15.2$ exists. We conclude
that $v_{\mathrm{opt}}$ (and hence $y_{\mathrm{opt}}$) is genuinely
transcendental in the usual sense: no rational-combination reduction to
elementary constants exists within the search radius.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[High-precision numerical values]
\label{cor:hp}
To 40 digits,
\[
\begin{aligned}
v_{\mathrm{opt}} &= 0.20283\,77447\,40492\,87031\,05459\,53643\,95496\,52206,\\
y_{\mathrm{opt}} &= 1.21585\,78321\,29242\,90943\,42098\,32081\,15330\,17778,\\
UB              &= 4.21851\,69938\,97451\,33111\,87703\,47651\,63097\,21854.
\end{aligned}
\]
\end{corollary}

\section{The Lower-Bound Side}
\label{sec:lb}

Czyzowicz et al.'s \Cref{czyzowicz2014} Theorem~7 states the lower bound
\[
T^*_{3,\mathrm{wireless}} \;\ge\; 1 + \tfrac{2}{3}\arccos\!\left(-\tfrac{1}{3}\right)
+ \tfrac{4\sqrt 2}{3} \;\approx\; 4.15937,
\]
leaving a gap of $\approx 0.06$ against our \Cref{thm:main}. Their
argument (their Lemma~5 and~6) has the form: at time $1+x$, the total
scanned perimeter is $\le kx$, so the unexplored set has length
$\ge 2\pi - kx$; Lemma~5 then exhibits two unexplored points at
shorter-arc distance $\ge u := 2\pi - kx$; the adversary commits the exit
to whichever is reached second, forcing the \emph{finder}'s chord to be
$\ge 2\sin(u/2)$. Optimising over $x$ (with $k=3$) yields the closed
form above at $x_{\mathrm{opt,LB}} = \tfrac23 \arccos(-\tfrac13)$.

\begin{remark}[Where the LB argument has slack]
\label{rem:lb-slack}
The argument bounds the \emph{finder}'s chord to the exit, not the
bottleneck's. In $A_3(y_{\mathrm{opt}})$ at its worst exit
$\theta \approx 1.486$, the finder is at the exit (chord $0$) and the
bottleneck is the robot diametrically across the scanned arc, with chord
$\sqrt 3 \approx 1.732$ — strictly larger than the Czyzowicz et al.\
chord lower bound $2\sin(xk/2) \approx 1.586$ evaluated at the same $x$.
Tightening the LB thus requires arguing that the \emph{bottleneck} chord
exceeds $2\sin(xk/2)$ for every three-robot wireless algorithm, not only
the finder's. Verified via the Wolfram script
\texttt{analysis/czyzowicz\_lb.wls}.
\end{remark}

\section{Next Steps}
\label{sec:next}

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Richer algorithm class.} Retaining the $A_3$ skeleton
caps improvements at the $\approx 1/1280$ reported here. Natural
generalisations include: allowing $r_3$ to continue scanning after
redeployment (a fourth phase, not used by $A_3$); letting all three
robots perform a return-to-origin + redeploy (symmetric analogue);
curved (non-radial) approaches; and asymmetric role assignments that
break the $L_1 = L_2$ reflection symmetry. Preliminary numerical
experiments (\texttt{experiments/a3\_local\_search.py}) show no gain
from breaking the structural symmetries at fixed skeleton, so the next
productive step is a genuinely larger family.
\item \textbf{Lower bound.} As noted in \Cref{rem:lb-slack}, tightening
Czyzowicz et al.'s LB reduces to arguing that the bottleneck chord (not
the finder's) is bounded below. A promising route is a multi-exit
adversary that keeps three candidate exits alive simultaneously; if the
three candidates are forced to be mutually far, then by pigeonhole some
robot is far from the actual exit regardless of how the algorithm has
positioned its robots. Formalising this for $k=3$ is open.
\end{itemize}
